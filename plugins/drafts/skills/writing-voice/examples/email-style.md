# Concise Work Email

For professional email: client notes, internal coordination, delegation,
document comments, follow-ups, and quick replies. Use it when the message needs
to move work forward without sounding rushed, vague, or over-polished.

## Voice

Clear, practical, and low-ceremony. The email should sound like the writer is
already inside the work: naming the answer, ask, decision, recommendation,
blocker, or risk before adding background.

Warmth comes from respect for the reader's time. A brief acknowledgement,
specific thanks, or clean close is enough when the situation calls for it.
Avoid padding the message to make it feel more formal.

Keep confidence proportionate. Say what is known, what is a judgment call, and
what still needs checking. Do not soften a real decision until the reader has
to infer it.

## Moves That Matter

- Lead with the live move: answer, ask, decision, recommendation, blocker, or
  next step.
- Add only the context that changes what the reader should do next.
- When tasking someone, name the output, source or constraint, check that
  matters, and timing.
- Push back through the decision criterion, not taste or vague preference.
- Repair messy work by naming the next edit, not by over-explaining the
  problem.

## Shape

Default movement:

1. Useful move.
2. Necessary context.
3. Action, owner, timing, check, or clean stop.

Short paragraphs work best. Lists are useful for multiple asks, comment points,
handoffs, or checks, but ordinary emails should not become workplans. Quick
replies can be fragments when the thread already carries the context.

Open with what the reader can use. End once the next move is visible.

## Language

Prefer plain, specific wording: file, deck, section, workbook, source, owner,
ask, decision, assumption, variance, blocker, timing, and check.

Use direct verbs: confirm, update, check, add, remove, reconcile, flag,
circulate, prepare, revise, discuss, pause, fold, cut, and frame.

Use qualifiers only when they narrow uncertainty: "I think," "likely," "not
sure," "for context," "my view," "as discussed," and "if helpful." A qualifier
should not weaken a clear call.

Avoid abstract business filler unless the thread itself requires the term:
leverage, unlock, robust, seamless, transformative, strategic alignment,
optimize, enable, value-add, and best-in-class.

## Cadence And Punctuation

Short sentences are good for confirmations, decisions, and firm judgments.
Split long sentences when the ask, reason, and timing start competing.

Bullets are for concrete execution: owners, checks, options, open questions, or
timing. Keep them parallel enough that the reader can scan them.

Avoid exclamation marks in ordinary work email. Avoid slashes when a word is
clearer. Do not hide the main request in parentheses.

## Examples

Example: "Confirmed. I will update the deck and send the revised version before
3."
Why it works: it answers first, names the action, and gives timing without
extra ceremony.

Example: "The blocker is the source file. I can update the deck once we confirm
whether to use the latest version or keep the prior reported totals unchanged."
Why it works: it names the dependency and avoids inventing approval.

Example: "I agree with the direction. The only thing I would tighten is the
handoff between the market context and the recommendation."
Why it works: it confirms, narrows the issue, and identifies the edit.

Prefer: "Can you please update the workbook with the latest assumptions and
flag anything that moves EBITDA by more than 5%?"
Avoid: "Would you be able to take a look at the workbook and make any updates
you think are relevant?"

Prefer: "This reads more like background than a recommendation. I would tie it
to the decision the client needs to make next."
Avoid: "Make this more actionable."

## Avoid

- Pattern: ceremonial opener before the point.
  Fix: start with the action or decision, then add context.
- Pattern: vague delegation.
  Fix: name the output, source, check, timing, and escalation path.
- Pattern: invented warmth.
  Fix: use a concrete acknowledgement or no warmth at all.
- Pattern: softened criticism that hides the issue.
  Fix: name what is off and give the narrower correction.
- Pattern: abstract consulting filler.
  Fix: use the actual file, owner, assumption, variance, or decision.

## Modes

### Client Or External

Use when: writing to a client, external collaborator, or broader professional
audience.

Default movement:

1. Light greeting or acknowledgement.
2. Purpose, answer, or recommendation.
3. Brief rationale or context.
4. Clear next step or timing.

What changes: add enough framing that the message feels considered.
What stays stable: directness, concrete context, and visible next step.

Example: "Thanks, this is helpful. I think the cleanest path is to keep the
scope focused on the operating model questions for now, then treat the tooling
discussion as a follow-up once we have alignment on process."
Why it works: it acknowledges the input, gives a recommendation, and explains
the sequencing.

Boundary: use more context when the reader has not been part of the thread.

### Internal Decision Traffic

Use when: coordinating with colleagues who already know the work.

Default movement:

1. Answer or judgment.
2. One reason or caveat.
3. Action, owner, or decision needed.

What changes: the message can be faster and more fragment-friendly.
What stays stable: the decision criterion still needs to be visible.

Example: "I think this is enough for the first pass. The open question is
whether we want the client to react to the process view or the technology view
first."
Why it works: it gives a judgment and isolates the live decision.

Boundary: add more framing before forwarding outside the immediate team.

### Delegation Or Request

Use when: asking someone to produce, check, update, or send something.

Default movement:

1. Name the output.
2. Name the source or constraint.
3. Name the check that matters.
4. Name timing or escalation path.

What changes: the ask carries more task detail.
What stays stable: the message still starts from the work, not from a long
setup.

Example: "Can you please update the deck with the revised timeline, check that
the milestones match the workbook, and flag anything that changes the story
before 2?"
Why it works: it gives output, source, check, and timing in one clean ask.

Boundary: use fewer details only when the owner already has the full task.

### Document Comment

Use when: commenting on decks, memos, workbooks, or draft sections.

Default movement:

1. Name what the current text is doing.
2. Name the issue or decision criterion.
3. Suggest the fix.

What changes: the note diagnoses before rewriting.
What stays stable: the fix is concrete enough to act on.

Example: "This is directionally right, but it reads like background. I would
turn it into the implication for the client: what decision does this force?"
Why it works: it names the problem and gives the rewrite principle.

Boundary: do not over-explain when an inline comment needs only a surgical fix.

### Quick Reply

Use when: the thread only needs confirmation, receipt, approval, or a small
next step.

Default movement:

1. Confirm.
2. Add action or timing only if needed.

What changes: fragments are fine.
What stays stable: do not add filler to make the reply look complete.

Example: "Confirmed, will send shortly."
Why it works: the thread already carries the context.

Boundary: do not use this mode when the other person needs rationale or a
decision.

## Revision Checks

- Does the first line carry the answer, ask, decision, recommendation, or
  blocker?
- Is the background limited to what changes the reader's next move?
- If the draft tasks someone, are the output, source or constraint, check, and
  timing visible?
- If the draft pushes back, does it name the real criterion and the better
  path?
- Is the tone direct but not cold: light warmth, no ceremony, no fake softness?
- Did vague wording become concrete objects, files, decisions, or risks?
- Are qualifiers narrowing uncertainty instead of weakening a clear call?
- For external readers, is there enough framing to feel considered?
- For internal readers, is the note compressed without becoming cryptic?
- Did the draft cut quoted-thread residue, notification language, signatures,
  and topic jargon that is not part of the live message?
- Does it stop once the recipient knows what to do next?
