# Professional Style

For executive reports, market-facing thought leadership, board-style narrative,
proposal sections, and strategy decks. Based on brand strategy and tone of
voice guidance, house editorial rules, and approved professional-report
examples. Use when professional writing needs a clear advisory point of view
and careful house discipline. Voice shapes the argument; house rules control
mechanics, terminology, entity names, and claims.

## Voice

Write with smart, clear, confident professionalism. The style should feel expert
without arrogance, accessible without becoming casual, and positive without
overclaiming.

The writing earns trust by turning facts into insight, then insight into a
practical opportunity, choice, or implication. It should show a point of view,
but the point of view must be supported by evidence, experience, or a concrete
business reason.

## Moves That Matter

- Lead with an insight, not a general market setup.
- Turn complexity into a clear decision frame, sequence, or operating model.
- Use evidence to interpret, not merely to display data.
- Make the opportunity specific: who can act, what changes, and why it matters.
- Use metaphor only when it organizes the argument. Keep it consistent and
  useful, as in a report that carries one governing analogy across sections.
- Keep claims positive but qualified. Prefer "can help," "may support," and
  "is designed to" when the outcome depends on context or execution.

## Shape

For executive thought leadership:

1. State the insight or tension.
2. Explain why it matters now.
3. Interpret the evidence.
4. Name the opportunity, trade-off, or decision.
5. End with a practical implication or next question.

For strategy decks and reports:

1. Use section titles that name the action or conclusion.
2. Give each section one controlling idea.
3. Pair data with an interpretation.
4. Move from strategic framing to execution detail.
5. Make dependencies, risks, and sequencing visible.

Avoid starting with generic change language. The reader should quickly see the
specific issue, the commercial implication, and the recommended frame.

## Language

Use precise business language. Prefer "value creation," "portfolio strategy,"
"operating model," "separation," "governance," "capability," "risk,"
"execution," "buyer," "seller," "asset," "business continuity," and
"trade-off" when those terms fit the subject.

Use everyday language around the technical core. Do not make the reader fight
through long chains of abstract nouns.

Claims should be specific and supportable:

- Prefer: "Early value creation planning can help more of the improvement price
  into the deal."
- Avoid: "Early planning guarantees maximum value."
- Prefer: "The better question is which playbook fits the asset, buyer, and
  separation risk."
- Avoid: "Organizations should urgently transform their carve-out approach."

Avoid unsupported superiority or promissory terms such as "best,"
"unmatched," "guaranteed," "ensure," "world-class," "market-leading," and
"seamless" unless approved proof and context are provided.

## House Rules

- Use US English unless the artifact has a local-market requirement.
- Use sentence case for most headings and titles unless a proper noun or
  official name requires different casing.
- Avoid unnecessary capitalization.
- Define acronyms on first use when the audience may not know them.
- Use exact organization, entity, service, product, sector, and geography names.
- Use ampersands only in official names, service labels, or approved compact
  surfaces.
- Use contractions selectively. They can make articles and letters more
  conversational, but avoid them in formal or compliance-sensitive copy.
- Keep bullets parallel. Use punctuation for full-sentence bullets; short
  phrase bullets can usually stay unpunctuated.
- Claims about performance, superiority, savings, risk reduction, compliance,
  client outcomes, or results must be factual, accurate, and supportable.
- Legal, independence, privacy, and claims guidance override voice preferences.

## Cadence And Punctuation

Use concise, controlled sentences. Short sentences can land a point of view.
Longer sentences should connect claim, evidence, caveat, and implication.

Punctuation should clarify the argument. Colons are useful for a reveal,
criterion, or list. Questions should name real business decisions, not create
drama. Avoid repeated dashes and exclamation marks in professional copy.

## Examples

Example: "A carve-out is not won at signing. The value case is built earlier,
when portfolio choices, separation risk, and operational upside are still
visible enough to shape the deal."
Why it works: it leads with an insight, creates a decision frame, and keeps the
claim tied to execution.

Example: "The issue is not whether the asset can stand alone. The issue is
which standalone path protects business continuity while giving buyers enough
confidence to price the upside."
Why it works: it reframes the question around the trade-off that matters.

Prefer: "Carve-outs reward sellers that identify complexity and value levers
early."
Avoid: "In today's complex transaction environment, carve-outs present both
challenges and opportunities for sellers."

Prefer: "The relay frame works because each handoff changes the risk profile."
Avoid: "The relay metaphor provides a compelling lens through which to
conceptualize transaction execution."

## Avoid

- Pattern: generic macro setup.
  Fix: name the specific pressure, asset issue, or decision.
- Pattern: data without interpretation.
  Fix: explain what the data changes for the reader.
- Pattern: clever metaphor that does not organize the argument.
  Fix: keep one useful analogy or remove it.
- Pattern: confidence without proof.
  Fix: qualify the claim or add the supporting evidence.
- Pattern: house rules treated as tone.
  Fix: apply naming, mechanics, terms, and claims constraints explicitly.
- Pattern: transaction terms carried into unrelated subjects.
  Fix: use transaction vocabulary only when the current artifact is actually
  about that topic.

## Modes

### Executive Report

Use when writing a substantial market-facing report or board-style narrative.

Default movement:

1. Insight or tension.
2. Evidence and interpretation.
3. Decision frame or opportunity.
4. Execution implication.

What changes: allow a stronger narrative frame and more developed argument.
What stays stable: precise claims, clear sequencing, and evidence-led optimism.

### Strategy Deck

Use when writing slide titles, section openers, or executive summaries.

Default movement:

1. Title the conclusion.
2. State the supporting fact or reason.
3. Name the implication for action.

What changes: compress aggressively and make each line earn its slide space.
What stays stable: no generic setup, no unsupported claims, no decorative
language.

### Professional Email Or Note

Use when adapting the style for direct professional communication.

Default movement:

1. State the useful point.
2. Add the necessary context.
3. Give the action, decision, or open question.

What changes: reduce the formal report cadence.
What stays stable: clarity, proportional confidence, and exact wording.

## Revision Checks

- Does the opening contain a real insight, tension, or decision?
- Is each data point interpreted rather than merely reported?
- Are claims proportionate and supportable?
- Are entity names, acronyms, geography, and service terms exact?
- Is any metaphor doing structural work?
- Does the piece point to an opportunity, implication, or choice without
  becoming instructional?
