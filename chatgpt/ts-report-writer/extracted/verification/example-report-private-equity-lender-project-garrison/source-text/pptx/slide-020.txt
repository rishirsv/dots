New On-book Volume and NBMs by Product
Target classifies on-book volume into the following categories: SSLP, MMGP, CFL, Upper Mid Cap and ABL. In general, NBM on SSLP and MMGP loans is lower (between 2.0% and 3.0%) than NBM on CFL and Upper Mid Cap loans (between 4.0% and 5.5%). There has been a general shift away from Upper Mid Cap loans and towards SSLP and MMGP loans, which has reduced portfolio-wide yields. The shift can also be seen in the change in revenue mix from 2013 (when Core made up 70% of total revenue and SSLP/MMGP 21%) to 2014 (Core: 66% of total revenue, SSLP/MMGP: 24%). ABL loans, on which NBM varies quarter-to-quarter based on non-market-specific factors (such as borrower and collateral credit quality), made up an insignificant proportion of new on-book volume in 2014.
We recommend that, in addition to market-based considerations such as receptiveness to and growth opportunities associated with certain product types, you consider the NBM associated with each product type in determining a target asset mix post-transaction.
New On-book Volume and NBMs by Deal Type
New on-book volume has been driven largely by new deals (>50% of total on-book volume in 2014), followed by
refinancings
(>25% of total on-book volume in 2014) and line increases (<20%). Top-ups, which are infrequent (less than $100 million in 2014), make up the remainder. There are exceptions in this trend from quarter-to-quarter; for example,
refinancings
drove more than 50% of new on-book volume in Q2’14. NBM for line increases tends to be the highest, presumably because such line increases are on original loans that are older and therefore priced higher. New deals have the lowest NBM given the lower interest rates currently prevalent in the credit markets. NBM for
refinancings
trends between line increases and new trends.
After a period of decline from 2011 to 2013, when new deals as a percentage of all deals decreased from 70% to 59%, new deals as a percentage of all deals rose sharply to 76% in 2014. While this trend is consistent between SSLP and non-SSLP deals, in general, new deals make up a higher proportion of all SSLP deals (77% on average over the past 5 years) than non-SSLP deals (58% on average over the past 5 years).
In addition to factors discussed elsewhere, the predominance of new deals (which command the lowest NBM) as a source of new on-book volume has depressed portfolio-wide yield. We recommend that, in addition to considering the strategic focus and expertise of the origination team, you consider the impact on portfolio yield in determining a strategic direction with respect to sources of new on-book volume.
