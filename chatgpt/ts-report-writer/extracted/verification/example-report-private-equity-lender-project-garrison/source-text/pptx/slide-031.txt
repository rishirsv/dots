Asset quality
Loans in non-accrual status have steadily decreased from $743 million as at December 31, 2010 to $216 million as at March 31, 2015. Net charge-offs have also declined from 122 bps to 32 bps over the same period. Management attributes the higher net charge-offs in 2010 and 2011 to the economic downturn and industry changes within the TMT portfolio (specifically the impact of the internet on paper-based media companies). Target experienced significant recoveries during 2013 and more are projected for 2015 from long charged-off accounts.
In 2014, Target changed its loan charge-off policy whereby loans are written off when deemed uncollectible within the quarter identified based on management judgment and evaluation of available data. Previously, loans were written off within the year identified. The change in policy had no impact to earnings. For financing receivables previously subject to a write-off, the written off amount may not be adjusted up to the original loan balance for any recoveries. Instead, recoveries are recorded as an offset to charge-offs and therefore reduce additions to the provision.
Examples of items considered by management when determining whether or not an account should be written off include: liquidation proceedings substantially concluded; collateral securing loan has been repossessed/sold; collection costs exceed the collectable amount; fraud or deceased borrower. Bankruptcy does not trigger an automatic charge-off.
Loans are evaluated for impairment collectively (e.g. general reserve) and individually (e.g. specific reserve) on a monthly basis. If
a loan is determined to be impaired, a
charge to earnings
is calculated as the difference between the recorded book value and the estimated recoverable amount. Target uses a
DCF Model, fair value of collateral, BEV Model, and observable market price in determining an estimated recoverable amount.
