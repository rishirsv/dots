Upfront Fees (continued)
Given that Target’s ability to generate upfront fees is predicated on the ability to lead syndications, we recommend that you consider Target’s ability to syndicate and to continue to assume lead arranger roles on these syndications post-transaction. This ability is driven by the total volume originated, the depth of Target’s sponsor relationships and the strength of its capital markets team. We note further that leading syndications affords a number of ancillary benefits, such as the ability to control asset portfolio quality and the forging of client / investor relationships, that generate incremental revenue or cost savings in the longer term.
Deferred Fee Amortization
Deferred
fee amortization includes both syndication fees and closing fees deferred and amortized into interest income over the stated life of the loan facility.
Deferred syndication fees are fees received from borrowers in syndicated transactions that relate to the portion of the total commitment retained by Target (excluding
any “above-market” fees, which are recognized upfront as noted above),
regardless of whether the syndicated transaction is classified as a “Loan Syndication” or a “Loan Participation” for accounting purposes.
Total deferred fees amortized into interest income in 2014 decreased $3 million, from $61 million in 2013 to $58 million in 2014. Deferred fees generally correlate with “hold” sizes (that is, the amount
of total volume retained on Target’s books). Since 2010, hold sizes have been decreasing (from 64% of volume originated in 2010 to 50% in 2014). The decline from 2013 to 2014 is driven primarily by a decrease in the percentage of originated volume retained from 57% to 50%.
We note that the distinction between syndication fees recognized upfront and fees deferred is essentially an accounting distinction. To the extent that cash fees deferred (both syndication fees and closing fees) in any given period approximate deferred fees amortized to income in the same period, it is useful to think of total fees in a given period as equal to the sum of amounts recognized upfront and deferred amounts amortized into income in the same period. In the absence of data relating to cash receipts and deferrals, we are unable to ascertain if fees deferred in a period do indeed approximate deferred fees amortized to income in the period.
