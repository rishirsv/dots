Portfolio yield
On a portfolio basis, yields have decreased over the historical period analyzed. Interest income yield in Q1’15 was 4.78%, compared to 4.95% in 2014 and 5.53% in 2013. Management’s yield calculations include the amortization of deferred fees (i.e., closing fees). The differential is directionally consistent but larger (62 bps from 2013 to 2014, and another 58 bps from 2014 to Q1’15) when such amortization is included in yield calculations.
The primary driver for this decline is the replacement of high-yield loans underwritten post-crisis by new or refinanced loans priced lower. Management states that this trend is expected to persist into 2017. We recommend that you consider how projected portfolio run-offs and the replenishment of the portfolio with lower-priced loans will impact yields over the forecast period, including if and when yields on new loans can be expected to stabilize.
Further contributing to this decrease is the growth in the SSLP and MMGP strategic investor programs. Interest income yield (excluding amortization of discounts) on SSLP loans is significantly lower than the corresponding portfolio-wide yield. Interest income yield on SSLP loans was 3.98% in 2013 versus 5.40% for the entire portfolio; in 2014, SSLP yield was 3.85% compared to 4.91% for the entire portfolio. These lower yields reflect the lower risk associated with these programs as a result of pooled first-loss mechanisms and other protection mechanisms that are built into the programs. The growth of these programs as a proportion of the total portfolio has exerted downward pressure on overall yield. It is our understanding SSLP and MMGP senior debt will not be included in your bid. Excluding the SSLP loans, the interest income yield on the portfolio was 6.10% in 2013 and 5.55% in 2014.
Fee income yield has been relatively flat from 2013 (2.84%) to 2014 (2.86%). While we have calculated fee income yield using average receivables as a base in order to provide a view on fee income that you can expect to generate from a given investment in funded assets, we note here that fee income is generated primarily through syndication and assets under management, not average receivables. With a given average receivables base, a decrease in volume syndicated or in assets under management post-transaction will result in lower fee income and lower fee income yields.
Total revenue yield on the portfolio, which includes interest income, fee income and other income (and is calculated with average receivables as a base), decreased 86 bps, from 9.43% in 2013 to 8.56% in 2014. The decrease is driven primarily by the decrease in interest income yields. Contributing further to this decrease is the decline in equity gains and the increase in FAS 91 expense allocations (due to a change in allocation policy / methodology over the historical period).
