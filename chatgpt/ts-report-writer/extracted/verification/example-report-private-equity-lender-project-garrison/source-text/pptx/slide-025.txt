Management Fees
Management fees are paid by partners in SIPs to compensate Target for sourcing and servicing loans in these programs. These fees are driven by the volume
of
assets being managed on behalf of strategic investors and the agreed fee rate.
Management fees increased from $54 million in 2013 to $70 million in 2014. The increase was driven by
the
increase in assets in the SSLP program (which contributed an additional
$11 million of fees), the MMGP program (an additional $1 million of fees) and other SIPs excluding
Mubadala
($4 million in incremental fees)
.
Management fee rates (charged
on assets under management) are 50 bps for SSLP and
Mubadala
assets, 37.5 bps for MMGP and between 25 and 50 bps on other SIPs.
Management stated that there are growth opportunities in existing programs (MMGP; potential increase in SSLP capacity) and further opportunities (GSAM
SIP, new revolver-only programs with a sovereign wealth fund and a state pension plan
) are being negotiated.
To the extent that certain SIPs are excluded from the contemplated transaction (i.e., Golden Hill CLO I, LLC), management fees from these SIPs will no longer be earned. We recommend that you consider this as part of your valuation model.
Other Fees
Other fees principally include customer service fees (primarily annual
administrative fees, a set amount for each loan and typically $50,000 per year, but also fees charged to borrowers for re-pricing, waivers and amendments) and letter of credit fees (fees charged by Target to compensate Target for off-balance-sheet guarantees). Customer service fees, which are primarily a product of total on-book volume, have been relatively flat from 2013 ($29 million) to 2014 ($26 million), reflecting the relatively flat on-book volume over the period. Letter of credit fees have also been flat over the same period ($16 million and $17 million, respectively), presumably due to a flat notional amount relating to letters of credit provided (notional amount was $0.3 billion as at March 31, 2015).
Due to system limitations, Management has been unable to provide data on these fees prior to 2012.
Sweeps
Sweeps represent
unamortized deferred fees that are “swept” into interest income typically when a loan prepays, or in certain cases when it is refinanced / restructured in a manner that represents a “more than only minor” modification under relevant
accounting standards
. In general, a modification to an existing loan is consider “more than only minor” if the
cash flows associated with a loan change substantially (> 10% compared to the original loan).
Unamortized amounts swept
into interest income
increased from $29 million in 2013 to $42 million in 2014. Refinancing
/ restructuring of loans is more prevalent during times of declining interest rates, when borrowers can derive considerable benefit from renegotiating loan terms.
