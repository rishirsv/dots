Hyperion (continued):
Management stated any deals not assigned a “BSLA” tag are aggregated in an “unallocated balance” within Hyperion, which is monitored by the FP&A group at the Parent. The Parent-level FP&A group works closely with their business-level counterparts to clear the unallocated balance on a weekly basis. In addition, weekly management meetings are held at the business level to discuss both on-book deals and deals in the pipeline. These meetings are attended by the originations team and are intended, in part, to ensure that all closed deals are accurately reflected in Target’s financial records.
Tie-out of Financial Information
In 2012 and 2013, changes to GL account usage and chart of accounts mapping were introduced. GL account usage was improved by separately recording various balances that were historically aggregated in a single general ledger account. Hyperion is a dynamic system in which mapping and account usage changes implemented in the present are retrospectively applied to historical balances. Given that historical balances (2007 to 2011) were often aggregated, this retrospective application reduced the reliability of historical balances as reported in Hyperion reports. Accordingly, management presentations and / or management judgment were relied upon to determine certain balances in 2007 through 2011.
Balances relating to the SSLP / MMGP portfolio and the
Mubadala
JV are prepared by dedicated controllership teams for these portfolios and submitted to Target management. These submissions form the basis of the split between the Core-portfolio balances and those relating to these portfolios. Within the Core-portfolio, ABL balances were calculated using the ABL asset base (which was extracted from Hyperion) and ABL spreads for each period in question.
Specific procedures performed over Target’s financial information are as follows:
2012 to 2014
Target management extracted live versions of Hyperion system-generated reports for the years 2012 through 2014. We were able to agree reported balances as per Target’s detailed financial statements directly to Hyperion system-generated reports (including the ABL / non-ABL asset base split) with the following minor adjustments:
LIBOR floors on certain assets provide an income benefit in periods when the LIBOR on these assets is lower than the floor. This income benefit, which is tracked separately for internal management reporting purposes, is included in “Other Income” in the Hyperion reports. This amount was reclassified from “Other Income” to “Interest Income” in Target’s reported financial information. The amounts reclassified were less than $2 million in any given year. We were able to agree the adjustment to the specific GL account in which the balance resides
.
Certain upfront fees are reported as part of “Other Income” for regulatory purposes since such fees do not meet the definition of upfront fees for regulatory purposes. To ensure that reported upfront fees are consistent with the accounting definition of such fees (as opposed to the regulatory definition), these amounts reported in “Other Income” were reclassified to “Upfront Fees”. The amounts reclassified were $1 million in 2012 and 2013 and $22 million in 2014. We were able to agree the adjustment to the specific GL accounts in which the balance resides.
