# Writing standards and defensibility

These standards are designed to keep FDD writing **client-ready, defensible, and easy to review**.

They are consistent with the bundled diligence and IC writing guidance:
- Be factual and balanced; do not minimize risks; credibility matters.
- Use tables for financials, not just prose.
- Ask for missing inputs rather than inventing details.
- Maintain a running red-flag and open-items view.

## Tone

- **Factual and balanced**: Present both positives and risks without salesy language.
- **Specific**: Prefer defined periods, units, and quantified impacts when data exists.
- **Explicit about uncertainty**: If something is management-provided, estimated, or not verified, label it.

Avoid:
- Casual language (“pretty good”, “a lot of”, “huge”).
- Unqualified absolutes (“no risk”, “fully normalized”) unless evidence is clear.

## Claim discipline

Each section should answer a clear question, and each subsection should contain:
- **What we observed** (fact, supported)
- **What it implies** (interpretation, labeled)
- **What we recommend** (action or next step)

For exhibits:
- Title should state the takeaway or the question being answered.
- Every exhibit needs: **period, units, and source/basis**.

## Facts vs judgments

Use clear labeling:
- **Facts**: “Based on [source], EBITDA was $X in FY2025.”
- **Judgments**: “We view the sustainability of this margin improvement as uncertain because [reason].”

## Sourcing and basis conventions

For any material quantitative claim, provide a basis line such as:
- “Source: trial balance extract dated YYYY-MM-DD.”
- “Source: management adjustment schedule; not audited.”
- “Source: audited financial statements FY2023–FY2025.”

If the basis is not available, do not invent numbers. Add it to the request list.

## Defensibility rules for adjustments

When describing earnings adjustments:
- State **what** the adjustment is.
- State **why** it is being treated as non-recurring or run-rate.
- State **how** it was quantified (what data, which period, any assumptions).
- State **residual risk** and what could change the conclusion.

## Consistency rules

Maintain consistency across the document:
- Units ($, $k, $m) and sign conventions (negatives in parentheses).
- Period definitions (FY vs LTM vs quarterly).
- Metric definitions (what counts as working capital, what is included in EBITDA).

If definitions vary by exhibit, explicitly note the difference.

## Open items and escalation

Maintain a running list of:
- Open items / data requests
- Red flags found
- Items gating a conclusion (P0)

If a red flag is discovered:
- Describe what was found
- Severity (deal-breaker / significant / manageable)
- Potential mitigant and next step
- Potential impact on valuation or terms (qualitative if numbers unavailable)

## Style mechanics

- Use headings and subheadings (avoid long unstructured paragraphs).
- Use bullets for clarity; limit nested bullets to one level unless necessary.
- Keep the “so what” in the first 1–2 sentences of each section (exec readability).
