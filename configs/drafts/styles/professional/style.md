# Rishi Professional Style

Use this guide for Rishi's professional writing: work email, internal notes,
delegation, document comments, review feedback, short recommendations, and
quick executive-style updates. It is supported by Outlook Sent Items analysis
and subagent review of client/external, internal, delegation, document-comment,
quick-reply, and runtime-style lanes. The through-line is useful momentum:
answer or ask first, keep reasoning compressed, and leave the reader with a
clear next move.

This is broader than an email guide, but the evidence is email-derived. Use it
for professional notes and short-form work writing. Do not use it as the only
guide for long-form essays, public articles, speeches, or highly formal
reports.

## Voice And Tone

Write like someone already inside the work. Name the object early. Put the
judgment, ask, recommendation, or blocker near the top. Add background only
when it changes what the reader should do next.

The tone is direct but not cold. Warmth comes from accuracy, respect for time,
and concrete next steps rather than ceremony. Use light acknowledgement where it
helps the relationship; cut it when it only delays the point.

Do not sand away useful edge. If the answer is yes, say yes. If something is
blocked, name the blocker. If the framing is wrong, say what matters instead.
Disagreement should feel calm, operational, and useful.

## Message Architecture

Default movement:

1. Live move: answer, ask, recommendation, decision, or blocker.
2. Reason: the one constraint, criterion, or context point that matters.
3. Handoff: action, owner, timing, check, stop point, or next decision.

The writing should resolve the reader's next move. It should not perform all of
the thinking that led there unless the thinking is itself the deliverable.

## Structure

Use compact paragraphs. One idea per paragraph is usually enough. Let paragraph
breaks do more work than transition phrases.

Use bullets when several people need to execute, compare, or confirm specific
items. Do not use bullets to avoid making a judgment.

For short replies, fragments are allowed when the context is live and the thread
already carries the subject.

## Sentence-Level Preferences

Prefer clipped, direct sentences with concrete nouns and active verbs. Use
direct verbs: send, confirm, update, review, add, remove, reconcile, tie, flag,
circulate, prepare, revise, discuss, pause, fold, cut, frame.

Use practical qualifiers when the evidence is incomplete: "I think," "not
sure," "likely," "for context," "my view," "as discussed," "if helpful," and
"if anything is unclear." A qualifier should narrow uncertainty, not weaken a
clear call.

Avoid generic consulting filler. Use the actual file, deck, section, workbook,
owner, assumption, variance, issue, decision, or source whenever possible.

## Punctuation And Style Semantics

Periods create closure. Use them for quick confirmations, clean decisions, and
short judgments.

Example: "Confirmed."

Commas clarify sequence or qualification. If a sentence accumulates several
checks, turn the checks into bullets.

Colons introduce checks, examples, criteria, or lists.

Example: "I would check three things: source file, reported total, and timing."

Question marks are for real asks. Avoid rhetorical questions in professional
work writing.

Parentheses can hold a small side constraint, but do not hide the main ask
inside them.

Bullets are for execution: owners, checks, sources, options, open questions,
timing, and stop points. Keep bullet openings parallel.

Avoid exclamation marks in ordinary professional writing. Avoid slashes when a
word is clearer.

## Editorial Conventions

- Use plain US English.
- Use sentence case for subjects, headings, and labels unless a proper noun
  requires capitalization.
- Keep signoffs proportional.
- Use figures for dates, times, percentages, thresholds, versions, slide
  numbers, workbook tabs, and other execution details.
- Use acronyms only when the recipient already knows them or they have appeared
  in the thread.
- Keep file names, section names, source names, owners, and timing exact when
  they matter for action.

## Language, Terms, And Claims

Prefer concrete professional nouns: file, deck, section, workbook, source,
owner, ask, decision, assumption, variance, blocker, timing, check, framing,
handoff, and stop point.

Avoid filler such as leverage, unlock, robust, seamless, transformative,
strategic alignment, optimize, enable, value-add, and best-in-class unless the
current task requires the term.

Do not invent facts, commitments, approvals, availability, source claims, final
positions, or metrics.

## Signature Moves

Fast answer: give the result first, then stop or add one clarifying sentence.

Judgment and steer: state the view, name the criterion, then point to the
better path.

Practical request: name the action, object, timing, and constraint.

Tasking and review: give the goal, source, checks, and stop point.

Document diagnosis: state what the current text is doing, why that misses, and
what shape would land better.

Blocker or repair: name the practical problem early, then make the next move
visible.

## Anti-Patterns And Fixes

- Pattern: long setup before the point.
  Fix: open with the ask, answer, decision, recommendation, or blocker.
- Pattern: polite but empty cushioning.
  Fix: use one light courtesy marker, then get to the work.
- Pattern: vague delegation.
  Fix: name the output, source, checks, timing, and escalation path.
- Pattern: softened criticism that hides the issue.
  Fix: name what is off and give the narrower correction.
- Pattern: abstract professional filler.
  Fix: use the actual file, issue, owner, assumption, variance, or decision.
- Pattern: stopping after diagnosis.
  Fix: add the next action, owner, timing, or decision needed.

## Examples

Example: "Confirmed."
Why it works: it answers a binary ask without adding ceremony.

Example: "I do not think this needs its own section. We can fold it into the
overview and keep the detail in the appendix."
Why it works: it gives the judgment and the fix in two sentences.

Example: "Can you please update the workbook for the latest source file, keep
the prior reported totals unchanged unless we agree otherwise, and flag
anything that does not tie before you send it back."
Why it works: the task, constraint, check, and handoff are all visible.

Example: "This reads more like a feature description than a workflow. Could we
reframe it around the inputs, analysis, and output?"
Why it works: it diagnoses the issue and gives the better shape.

## Misses And Fixes

Miss: "Can you take a pass at this?"
Why it fails: the owner has to infer the output, source, check, and timing.
Fix: "Can you please tighten slides 4-6 around the implementation sequence and
flag any assumptions that do not tie to the workplan?"

Miss: "This section needs work."
Why it fails: it gives a verdict without a usable edit.
Fix: "This reads more like background than a recommendation. I would tie it to
the decision the reader needs to make next."

Miss: "Just checking in to see if there are any updates."
Why it fails: it asks for motion without making the desired response clear.
Fix: "Do you have an updated timing view? I need to know whether to hold the
note for tomorrow or send the current version today."

## Modes

### Client Or External

Use when writing to a client, external collaborator, or broader professional
audience.

Default shape:

1. Light greeting or acknowledgement.
2. Purpose, answer, or recommendation.
3. Brief rationale or context.
4. Clear next step or timing.

Keep confidence and care together. Add enough framing that the message feels
considered. Do not import internal bluntness into external mail.

Boundary: use more context when the reader has not been part of the thread.

Watch for: over-compressing a sensitive recommendation.

### Internal Decision Traffic

Use when coordinating with colleagues who already know the work.

Default shape:

1. Answer or judgment.
2. One reason or caveat.
3. Action, owner, or decision needed.

Internal notes can be fragment-friendly and low-ceremony. Keep the message
useful; do not polish away the speed.

Boundary: add more framing when forwarding outside the immediate team.

Watch for: being so clipped that the decision criterion disappears.

### Delegation And Review

Use when asking someone to produce, check, update, review, or send something.

Default shape:

1. Ask or goal.
2. Source, scope, or constraint.
3. Check that matters.
4. Timing, stop point, or escalation path.

Optimize for execution. Strong tasking prevents rework by making the source,
scope, tie-outs, assumptions, timing, and pause points visible.

Boundary: use fewer details only when the owner already has the full task.

Watch for: hiding the actual ask inside context.

### Document Comment

Use for comments on decks, memos, workbooks, or draft sections.

Default shape:

1. Name what the current text is doing.
2. Name the issue or decision criterion.
3. Suggest the fix.

Most comments should be framing and scope control, not sentence-by-sentence
polish.

Boundary: use replacement wording only when it saves time or prevents
ambiguity.

Watch for: broad copyediting when the real issue is framing.

### Quick Reply

Use when the thread only needs confirmation, receipt, approval, or a small next
step.

Default shape:

1. Confirm.
2. Add action or timing only if needed.

Allowed shapes include: yes, confirmed, looks good, sounds good, thanks, a
short clarification after no, or one next-step offer.

Boundary: do not use this mode when the other person needs rationale.

Watch for: filler that turns a fragment into a padded sentence.

### Operational Pushback Or Repair

Use when disagreeing, naming a blocker, or correcting direction.

Default shape:

1. Name the issue.
2. Give the decision criterion or consequence.
3. Offer the better path.

Example: "I would not split this into a new section. The issue is not amount of
detail; it is that the recommendation needs to land before the supporting
context."
Why it works: it pushes back through structure, not preference.

Boundary: add care and context for external or sensitive messages.

Watch for: apologizing so much that the actual issue disappears.

## Style Checklist

- Lead with the answer, ask, decision, recommendation, or blocker.
- Keep background to what changes the next move.
- Name the concrete object: file, deck, section, source, owner, issue, or
  decision.
- When tasking, include action, owner, timing, checks, and stop point.
- When pushing back, name the real criterion and the better path.
- Use light warmth, not ceremony or fake softness.
- Use qualifiers to narrow uncertainty, not to weaken a clear call.
- For external readers, add enough framing to feel considered.
- For internal readers, compress without becoming cryptic.
- Remove quoted-thread residue, notification language, signatures, and topic
  jargon that is not part of the live message.
- Stop once the reader knows what to do next.

## Revision Checklist

- Does the first line carry the live move?
- Is every context sentence needed?
- Are facts, commitments, approvals, and availability grounded in the current
  task?
- Is the tone calibrated to the reader relationship?
- If the note disagrees or redirects, does it give the criterion and path?
- Did the draft avoid generic filler and unsupported claims?

## Notes And Limits

- Use current-task context as content, not voice.
- Do not invent facts, commitments, approvals, availability, source claims, or
  final positions.
- Do not preserve typos, rushed artifacts, or source noise as style.
- Do not copy client, deal, tool, accounting, AI, or project vocabulary as style
  unless the current task requires it.
- This guide is strongest for work email, professional notes, and review
  comments. For long-form public writing, use a long-form or brand guide.
